Rooted in Our Red Soil

Born into a humble family in the heart of Mwala Sub-County, Felix Wambua’s early life was defined by the realities of rural Kenya. He walked kilometers to a local public school, fully understanding the daily struggle for water, the anxiety of delayed rains, and the unyielding hope of parents who sacrifice everything for their children's education.

These formative years instilled in him a profound work ethic and an unbreakable bond with the people of Machakos. He didn't just learn about community resilience; he lived it.

After earning a scholarship to pursue Economics and Public Policy, Felix could have settled comfortably in the corporate sector. Instead, he returned home, dedicating the last 15 years to grassroots organization, youth mentorship, and civic accountability.

The Pillars of My Leadership

The principles that guide every decision, policy, and action.

Fierce Accountability

The Senate's primary role is oversight. I pledge to protect devolution by ensuring every shilling allocated to Machakos is accounted for and visibly improves lives.

Inclusive Empathy

Leadership must be accessible. From the mama mboga in Athi River to the farmer in Kangundo, my office will be a sanctuary for the voices of all our people.

Modern Innovation

We cannot solve tomorrow's problems with yesterday's tools. I advocate for data-driven policies, tech-enabled agriculture, and modern education frameworks.

A Track Record of Service

2012 - 2016

The Youth Empowerment Initiative

Founded a community-based organization that provided vocational training and micro-loans to over 5,000 young men and women across Machakos.

2017 - 2021

Public Policy Advisor

Served on national boards advising on devolved agricultural policies, ensuring Machakos farmers gained access to subsidized fertilizers and modern farming techniques.

2022 - Present

Machakos Accountability Forum

Spearheaded civic education drives, helping citizens understand county budgets and demanding transparency in local infrastructure projects.
